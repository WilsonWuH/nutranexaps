## The Short Answer Buyers Need First

If your team is auditing a phosphatidylserine manufacturer for Europe, the minimum workable checklist is:

1. Lock the exact route, use case, and order stage before the audit starts.
2. Verify that the manufacturer operates under documented hygiene and HACCP-based controls relevant to the route being quoted.
3. Confirm that the route can be traced cleanly from controlled file to batch and packaged drum.
4. Check whether document control, version handling, and change escalation are disciplined enough for first-order approval.
5. Review packaging and dispatch evidence against the real commercial plan, not only against a generic product description.
6. End with a written go, hold, or corrective-action decision shared by procurement and QA.

That is the core distinction buyers often miss. A product page can show that a manufacturer makes phosphatidylserine. A COA sample can show reporting style. A factory photo can show equipment. None of those items, by itself, proves that the first commercial order is ready for a Europe-facing approval path.

## Why Manufacturer Audit Is Its Own Buyer Task

### EU buyers still carry responsibility for what they place on the market

The strongest reason to audit the manufacturer is not bureaucracy. It is responsibility. The Commission's General Food Law page explains that the General Food Law Regulation provides the foundation for food law across the chain, while Article 18 of Regulation (EC) No 178/2002 establishes traceability across production, processing, and distribution. For an importer or brand owner, that means supplier qualification is not only a commercial preference. It is part of how the business shows that it knows what it is buying, from whom, and under what control framework.

That does not mean every phosphatidylserine project needs a lengthy on-site audit before a quotation can move forward. It does mean buyers should scale their audit to the decision they are making. A light early-stage screen may be enough for a shortlist. A first commercial order, distributor handoff, or customer-facing technical file usually needs a more deliberate review.

The Commission's food supplements page reinforces the same point from a different angle. Food supplements are marketed by food business operators and the harmonised rules aim to protect consumers and avoid misleading information. For phosphatidylserine buyers, that makes manufacturer qualification relevant before price comparison turns into a committed Europe supply path.

### A supplier profile is not the same as a controlled manufacturing system

Many first-order decisions fail because teams confuse evidence types. The supplier may have a professional website, product pages, export claims, and a usable COA sample. Those are helpful, but they are not the same as an auditable manufacturing system.

The audit question is narrower: **can the buyer verify that the manufacturer controls the exact phosphatidylserine route it is selling well enough for first-order approval?**

That is why manufacturer audit should be treated separately from:

- [novel-food route review](/resources/phosphatidylserine-novel-food-review-europe/)

## The Six-Part Audit Checklist for Phosphatidylserine Manufacturers

### Lock the exact PS route and order stage first

Start by fixing the commercial item in writing. Is the team auditing general phosphatidylserine, a soy route, or a sunflower route? Is the audit for shortlist screening, sample approval, first bulk order, or distributor onboarding? Is the intended use a food supplement, a functional food project, or a broader technical comparison?

If that scope is not fixed, the audit quickly becomes vague. Procurement may ask about one route, QA may review another, and the manufacturer may present general capability slides that do not answer the actual buying question.

The opening audit note should record:

- product route under review
- intended Europe-facing use
- order stage
- which documents are already in hand
- which unresolved questions the audit must close

That simple note prevents the audit from becoming a generic factory tour.

### Review hygiene and food-safety-management controls

Once the route is locked, the next question is whether the manufacturer can explain its food-safety-management logic clearly enough for a buyer review. Regulation (EC) No 852/2004 requires permanent procedures based on HACCP principles, and the Commission's guidance platform points to its food-safety-management-systems notice and 852/2004 guidance for how those controls are applied in practice.

The commercial takeaway is an inference from those sources: a phosphatidylserine manufacturer audit should test whether the operator can show a credible hygiene and hazard-control structure for the route being sold, even if the buyer is not performing a formal regulatory inspection.

Useful audit questions include:

| Audit checkpoint | What the buyer is looking for | What should trigger follow-up |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Process overview | A consistent explanation of how the quoted PS route is produced and controlled | Different teams describe different routes or stages |
| Hygiene controls | A clear explanation of how cleaning, personnel, and production hygiene are managed | Controls are described only at a very high level with no route context |
| HACCP-based logic | Evidence that hazards are identified and managed within a food-safety system | The answer stays at marketing level and never reaches process control |
| Quality review flow | A usable explanation of who reviews release data and when | No one can explain how QA connects to the batch-release path |

The goal is not to demand confidential manufacturing know-how. The goal is to see whether the manufacturer can explain its control system coherently and consistently.

### Check traceability from route to drum

A phosphatidylserine audit is weak if it never tests traceability. Regulation (EC) No 178/2002 makes traceability a core concept across the food chain. In practical buying terms, the audit should verify that the route under review can be connected from controlled product identity to batch-level records and finally to the packed goods the buyer may receive.

That means asking how the manufacturer connects:

- the quoted route and specification
- batch or lot identification
- COA generation or review workflow
- packaging unit and drum presentation
- dispatch documentation

This is where the audit should intersect with, but not duplicate, the later [Phosphatidylserine Lot Traceability Checklist](/resources/phosphatidylserine-lot-traceability-checklist/) and [Incoming Inspection and Warehouse Release Checklist](/resources/phosphatidylserine-incoming-inspection-warehouse-release/). Those pages help after goods are moving. The manufacturer audit asks whether the underlying traceability logic is credible before the first order is approved.

### Inspect document control and change handling

A strong manufacturer audit always checks how the file set is controlled. Buyers do not need every internal document. They do need confidence that the files they receive are current, route-specific, and handled consistently.

At minimum, the audit should test whether the manufacturer can manage:

- current specification control
- COA sample versus live-lot distinction
- route-specific file naming
- packaging and dispatch evidence tied to the commercial item
- change escalation when a route, source, or support document shifts

This is the point where many buyers discover that a supplier looks organised until version control is tested. One person sends a sample COA. Another sends a specification with a different route description. A third refers to a document pack from an older order. The problem is not always the quality of the ingredient. The problem is that the first-order file has no reliable control centre.

The safer method is to ask one direct question: **if something changes between sample approval and bulk dispatch, how will the buyer know which file remains current?**

That question connects naturally to [What Documents Should Buyers Request for PS Ingredients?](/resources/documents-for-ps-ingredients/), [Phosphatidylserine Supplier Onboarding](/resources/phosphatidylserine-supplier-onboarding/), and [Phosphatidylserine Annual Supplier Review](/resources/phosphatidylserine-annual-supplier-review/).

### Verify packaging, dispatch, and first-order practicality

A manufacturer audit should not stop at process and paperwork. The buyer also needs to know whether the first commercial order can move through packaging and dispatch in a way that matches the approved plan.

This matters because a technically acceptable phosphatidylserine route can still create avoidable friction if the commercial handling details are unclear. Buyers should verify:

| First-order point | Why it matters in the audit |
| --- | --- |
| Standard MOQ | Confirms the route is realistic for the intended launch or qualification stage |
| Drum format | Shows whether the packaging unit matches receiving and warehouse expectations |
| Label and batch presentation | Helps the buyer assess downstream receiving clarity |
| Dispatch evidence availability | Shows whether the supplier can support remote approval before shipment |
| Warehouse and loading discipline | Indicates whether the order can be prepared consistently for export handling |

For Nutranexa, the verified public baseline is useful here: the site states a **25 kg MOQ** and **25 kg net per drum** for phosphatidylserine, and it shows packaging and dispatch imagery that buyers can use as early evidence. That does not replace a lot-specific pre-shipment review, but it helps buyers decide whether the first-order handling model looks commercially workable.

### Close with a go, hold, or corrective-action list

The last step is the one many teams skip. An audit only adds value if it ends with a usable closeout.

The closeout should record one of three outcomes:

- **Go:** the manufacturer appears qualified enough for the next commercial stage.
- **Hold:** the route remains interesting, but the audit did not close one or more critical questions.
- **Corrective action needed:** the buyer identified specific gaps that must be answered before approval.

The corrective-action list should be short and concrete. For example:

- clarify whether the current specification belongs to soy route or sunflower route
- confirm how batch identity will be shown on release files and packed drums
- provide current packaging and dispatch evidence for the quoted order format
- explain how document version changes are communicated after sample approval

That is better than a vague note that the audit was "generally satisfactory." Procurement and QA need a decision they can actually use.

## Common Mistakes in Phosphatidylserine Manufacturer Audits

Most failures come from scope and closeout problems, not from the existence of the audit itself.

1. Auditing a generic "PS supplier" instead of one exact route and one exact order stage.
2. Confusing product marketing assets with auditable process-control evidence.
3. Checking COA format without testing traceability from route to packaged goods.
4. Ignoring document version control until the order is already moving.
5. Treating packaging and dispatch as warehouse details rather than part of first-order readiness.
6. Ending the review without a go, hold, or corrective-action record.

The practical fix is to keep one question central: **what evidence do we need to approve this exact phosphatidylserine manufacturer for this exact European buying stage?**

## FAQ

### Is a phosphatidylserine document pack enough without a manufacturer audit?

Not for a first commercial approval decision. A document pack can help with route and specification review, but the buyer still needs confidence in hygiene controls, traceability, document control, and first-order handling.

### Should a European buyer perform an on-site audit every time?

Not necessarily. The right depth depends on the buying stage and risk level. Early screening can be lighter, while first-order approval usually needs a more deliberate remote or on-site review with written closeout.

### What is the most important traceability question in a phosphatidylserine audit?

Whether the quoted route can be tied consistently from controlled specification to batch identity, release evidence, and packed drums without ambiguity.

### How is a manufacturer audit different from a novel-food or contaminant review?

Those reviews answer narrower legal or specification questions. A manufacturer audit tests whether the operator, records, and shipment path are controlled well enough for commercial approval.

### What should buyers ask Nutranexa for if the audit is still incomplete?

Request the exact route-specific specification path, current COA review support, manufacturing and dispatch evidence, packaging context, and clarification on how document changes are handled between qualification and shipment.
