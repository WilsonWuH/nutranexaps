## The Short Answer Buyers Need First

If your team is releasing or requalifying a phosphatidylserine lot, the practical answer is simple: build one controlled lot file that matches the approved commercial item to the shipment identity, the COA identity, the pack count, and the downstream approval decision.

At minimum, that file should let you answer seven questions quickly:

1. What exact phosphatidylserine route is under review?
2. Which supplier and which commercial item does the lot belong to?
3. Does the COA refer to the same lot or batch that is physically being moved or received?
4. Do the drum labels, pallet count, and packing list point to the same shipment identity?
5. Are the supporting specification and source-related files tied to the same product route?
6. Were any mismatches or exceptions recorded before release?
7. Who approved, conditionally approved, or blocked the lot?

If a buyer cannot answer those questions from one file set, the traceability workflow is incomplete even if the shipment looks normal.

## What Traceability Means in US and EU Ingredient Workflows

### EU buyers need one-step-back and one-step-forward clarity

EU traceability discussions often sound abstract until a buyer has to retrieve a file quickly. The practical reading of Article 18 is that a business should be able to identify from whom it received the food and to whom it supplied it. For phosphatidylserine teams, that translates into a release file that makes the supplier side and customer side legible without rebuilding the history by email.

In real commercial use, EU buyers usually need:

- a clear supplier identity
- a defined product route such as general PS, soy PS, or sunflower PS
- a lot or batch identity that can be connected to the shipment
- a record of where that lot is going next inside distribution or production

That is why traceability should not be treated as the same thing as specification review. Specification review answers, "Is this the product we meant to buy?" Traceability answers, "Can we prove which approved lot moved through which commercial path?"

### US buyers need a supplier file that supports FSVP and quality review

US ingredient buyers face a slightly different practical pressure. The FDA's FSVP framework is built around risk-based supplier verification, documented procedures, supplier approval, verification activities, corrective actions, and periodic reevaluation. For dietary supplement ingredients and components, modified requirements may apply in some circumstances, but that does not remove the need for organized records. It increases the importance of tying supplier verification and product identity together correctly.

That matters for phosphatidylserine because import teams often split responsibilities across procurement, compliance, contract manufacturing, and warehouse operations. One group handles supplier approval. Another checks the lot on arrival. Another maintains the product record used in formulation or customer files. If those records do not point back to the same lot identity, the team may have technically "reviewed" the supplier without proving traceability for the shipped material.

The FDA's dietary supplement CGMP guidance is also useful here because it emphasizes lot, batch, or control identification as a way to support complete manufacturing history through distribution. Even when the finished product is not yet in consumer packaging, the buyer logic is the same: use a lot-linked record set that lets the business track what was reviewed, what moved, and what was released.

## Continue the Checklist

### Lock the exact commercial item first

Start with the product definition before opening the shipment paperwork. Record the commercial item exactly as approved:

- product route: phosphatidylserine, soy phosphatidylserine, or sunflower phosphatidylserine
- supplier name
- agreed grade or assay if applicable
- intended destination market
- intended use, such as further distribution or supplement manufacturing

This step prevents a common traceability failure: the warehouse receives a phosphatidylserine lot under a generic product name, while QA approved a source-specific route and procurement filed the PO under a shortened commercial code.

If the lot belongs to a soy or sunflower route, keep that source identity visible in the lot file from the start. Do not assume a generic "PS" folder is sufficient.

### Match the COA to the lot that will actually move

The COA is often the first traceability checkpoint buyers think about, but it only works if it refers to the same lot that is being shipped, received, or released.

Use a simple review table like this:

| Checkpoint | What should match | Why it matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| COA lot or batch reference | Shipment lot identity | Prevents release based on the wrong document |
| Product name and source route | Approved commercial item | Stops source confusion across general, soy, and sunflower PS |
| Manufacture or test dates | The lot under review | Helps QA confirm the document belongs to the shipment period |
| Analytical scope | Current specification route | Avoids relying on a legacy or sample-only format |
| Document status | Sample, historical, or current lot | Keeps buyers from releasing against a non-current example |

This is where buyers should slow down. A sample COA is useful for understanding reporting style. It is not enough by itself to close traceability for a live lot unless the team has explicitly documented how current-lot evidence will be matched and approved.

### Check drum, pallet, and quantity identity

Lot traceability fails surprisingly often at packaging level. The COA may be correct, but the physical shipment details still need to line up with the controlled file:

- number of drums
- net weight per drum
- pallet count if relevant
- drum or pallet label references
- seal or dispatch-photo evidence if available

For phosphatidylserine, this is commercially useful because drum-count errors are easier to catch before release than after internal transfer. It is also where packaging proof becomes more than a marketing asset. If the supplier has visible packaging and dispatch references, they can support buyer review of what a standard shipment should look like before the lot reaches production or onward distribution.

### Build the lot cross-reference sheet

Many teams keep the right records but still make traceability hard because the lot data is scattered. The fix is simple: build a one-page cross-reference sheet or digital equivalent.

That sheet should connect:

- internal item code
- supplier item name
- source route
- PO number
- shipment or packing-list reference
- COA reference
- lot or batch number
- quantity received or released
- destination warehouse, customer, or production job

This single sheet is often the difference between a file that is technically complete and a file that is operationally usable.

## The Seven-Point Phosphatidylserine Traceability Checklist

### Keep support files tied to the same commercial route

Traceability is not only about lot numbers. It also depends on whether the support documents in the approval file belong to the same commercial route as the lot being moved.

For example, a buyer may have:

- a current phosphatidylserine specification
- a sunflower PS COA example
- a soy-related customer questionnaire
- a general product quotation

Each document may be real, but the file is still weak if they do not describe the same route. The rule for buyers is simple: lot identity and support-document identity should point to the same commercial item. When they do not, record the gap and stop treating the file as fully traceable.

This is one reason separate product paths matter. Source-specific product pages, COA examples, and document request flows help buyers avoid mixing general PS, soy PS, and sunflower PS support materials in one release packet.

### Record deviations before warehouse release

Do not hide traceability mismatches inside email chains. Put them into the lot file before release. Typical examples include:

| Mismatch type | Example | Correct response |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Identity mismatch | COA lot differs from drum label lot | Hold release until the supplier clarifies which lot applies |
| Route mismatch | Specification says general PS but shipment is sunflower PS | Reconfirm the quoted item and update approval status |
| Quantity mismatch | Packing list shows 10 drums, receiving count shows 9 | Investigate before transfer or resale |
| Document-status mismatch | Team used a sample COA instead of current-lot support | Request current controlled evidence and log the exception |

This step protects both procurement and QA. It makes the release decision defensible, and it reduces the risk that a later complaint or audit turns into a reconstruction exercise.

### Close the file with an approval decision

A traceability packet is incomplete until someone records the outcome. The decision should be short and explicit:

- released
- conditionally released pending one document
- held for clarification
- rejected or blocked

Add the approver, date, and the exact reason if the lot is not fully released. This sounds administrative, but it is the final link between the traceability evidence and the commercial action the business took.

## Where Traceability Breaks Down Even When the Product Looks Fine

Most traceability failures are not dramatic. They come from small commercial shortcuts:

1. The buyer relies on the last sample COA instead of the lot under shipment.
2. The warehouse receives a generic PS label while QA approved a source-specific route.
3. The packing list and drum count are checked, but no one ties them to the COA reference.
4. The business has support files, but they belong to a different commercial item than the lot being moved.
5. The team finds a mismatch but never records whether the lot was held, released, or conditionally accepted.

This is why traceability review should sit next to, not inside, incoming inspection. Incoming inspection looks at the material and shipment condition. Traceability review proves that the material under review is the exact lot the business intended to receive, store, release, or resell.

That distinction matters even more for cross-border work. EU buyers need supplier-to-customer clarity. US importers need a file that stands up to FSVP and internal quality review. Both groups benefit from one lot-centered packet rather than a loose bundle of documents.

## FAQ

### What documents should a buyer request to prove phosphatidylserine lot traceability?

Buyers usually need the current specification, the lot-linked COA or documented current-lot COA path, the packing list, drum or pallet identity, quantity confirmation, and a short approval record that shows whether the lot was released, held, or conditionally accepted.

### Is traceability review the same as incoming inspection?

No. Incoming inspection checks what arrived and whether the shipment condition is acceptable. Traceability review checks whether the approved commercial item, the COA, the lot code, the packaging identity, and the release decision all refer to the same phosphatidylserine lot.

### Why should buyers keep general PS, soy PS, and sunflower PS files separate?

Because source route affects which specification, COA, and support files belong in the release packet. Mixing them weakens the approval record and makes later investigation harder.

### Do US phosphatidylserine importers always follow the full standard FSVP path?

Not always. FDA materials explain that modified requirements may apply for certain dietary supplements and dietary supplement components in some circumstances. Even so, buyers still need organized records that tie supplier verification, specifications, and lot identity together.

### What is the biggest traceability mistake procurement teams make?

The most common mistake is assuming that a familiar supplier and a clean-looking shipment are enough. In practice, the weak point is usually the mismatch between the lot on the COA, the lot on the packaging, and the commercial item that QA or regulatory teams previously approved.
