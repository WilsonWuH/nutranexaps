## Why PS Label Declaration Is Its Own Buyer Task

### Phosphatidylserine is an other dietary ingredient and belongs in Supplement Facts

The first reason this deserves its own workflow is that phosphatidylserine is not just background marketing copy. FDA's Dietary Supplement Labeling Guide says other dietary ingredients without established Daily Values, including phosphatidylserine, must be listed in the Supplement Facts panel by common or usual name with the amount by weight per serving. FDA's claims guidance separately says a brand may state that a product contains phosphatidylserine outside the panel, but only when the statement gives the specific amount and does not imply a Daily Value that does not exist.

That means the label review should begin with panel placement, not with front-label copy. Buyers should be able to confirm the serving size, the declared amount per serving, the naming logic, and whether the route being sold is the route represented in the panel.

This is not theoretical. In a March 8, 2023 warning letter, FDA cited a supplement label that listed phosphatidylserine outside the Supplement Facts panel rather than inside it with the quantitative amount per serving. The business lesson is simple: do not assume a clean-looking mockup is structurally correct just because the ingredient name appears somewhere on the package.

### Source wording and soy allergen disclosure create a second review layer

The second reason PS label review deserves its own workflow is that source identity changes the file. FDA's dietary supplement labeling guidance says the source ingredient that supplies a dietary ingredient **may** be identified in the Supplement Facts label, and if it is identified there, it generally does not need to be listed again outside the panel.

Soy routes create one more layer because soy is a major food allergen under FDA's food-allergen framework. FDA's food-allergy materials say a major allergen can be declared by placing the food source in parentheses after the ingredient, or by using a separate **Contains** statement adjacent to the ingredient list. FDA's allergen Q&A for dietary supplements also says that when the allergen source is already declared within Supplement Facts, firms usually do not need to repeat it elsewhere, unless they choose to use a Contains statement and therefore must include all major allergens there.

That is why source wording and allergen review should be separated from generic specification approval:

| File type | What it can help answer | What it does not prove by itself |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Supplement Facts draft | Where PS appears, how much is declared, and whether source wording is shown | That the source route matches the quoted commercial item |
| Specification | Which source route and product identity are actually being supplied | That the label applies allergen disclosure correctly |
| Allergen statement | How the supplier addresses soy-source allergen questions | That the U.S. label artwork is using the source in the right place |
| Customer questionnaire | What a downstream brand or contract manufacturer expects to see | That the supplier and artwork teams are reading the same route |

## The Six-Part Workflow for Reviewing a PS Supplement Facts Label

### Lock the exact phosphatidylserine route and serving basis first

Before reviewing any label artwork, write down the exact route and serving basis in one approval note. Is the project using general PS, soy PS, or sunflower PS? Is the label being reviewed for one capsule, two capsules, one stick pack, or another serving format?

This matters because artwork often stabilizes faster than sourcing. QA may be reviewing a soy-source specification while marketing is already using a generic PS label. If the serving basis and source route are unstable, the panel cannot be reviewed as final.

The opening review note should usually record:

- exact product route
- serving size under review
- target amount of phosphatidylserine per serving
- whether the label is for early mockup, final artwork, or contract-manufacturer transfer
- which downstream party asked for the label file

### Put phosphatidylserine in the right place and use the right naming logic

The second step is structural. FDA's guidance says phosphatidylserine is an other dietary ingredient and should appear inside Supplement Facts with the amount by weight per serving and the no-Daily-Value footnote.

A buyer should check:

1. Is phosphatidylserine inside the Supplement Facts panel rather than treated as stray front-panel copy?
2. Is the amount per serving stated in metric units?
3. Does the panel use a stable name that matches the route decision?
4. Is **Daily Value Not Established** handled correctly for the PS line?

### Decide where source wording should appear for soy or sunflower routes

Once the panel placement is correct, decide where source wording belongs. FDA says the source ingredient may be identified in the Supplement Facts panel. In practical B2B terms, a U.S. team usually has three workable questions: should the panel say only **phosphatidylserine**, should it identify a source in parentheses, and if not, where will the ingredient list carry it?

For soy and sunflower routes, this is not a cosmetic preference. It is the point where procurement, regulatory, and customer-facing files meet.

The right answer depends on the exact project, but the review logic should stay disciplined:

| Review checkpoint | Buyer question |
| --- | --- |
| Route match | Does the proposed wording match the exact soy, sunflower, or general PS item under quotation? |
| Source placement | Is the source shown in Supplement Facts, the ingredient list, or both? |
| File consistency | Does the specification use the same route logic as the artwork? |
| Customer use | Is the wording strong enough for a downstream brand or co-manufacturer to rely on? |
| Revision control | If the source changes, will the label review reopen automatically? |

### Handle soy allergen disclosure without duplicating the file blindly

This is where many U.S. label reviews become messy. Soy-source PS projects often trigger a reflex to repeat soy in every possible place. FDA's food-allergy materials say the allergen source must appear at least once in a compliant manner. FDA's dietary-supplement allergen guidance explains that if the source ingredient or the allergen source is already declared within Supplement Facts, that usually satisfies the requirement without repeating it elsewhere. But if the brand uses a **Contains** statement, that statement must include all major allergens present.

For buyers, the practical question is not "how many times can we print soy?" The question is narrower:

- where is soy already declared
- does that declaration clearly connect to the PS route
- is the brand also using a Contains statement for broader allergen control
- will the contract manufacturer or customer require one form rather than another

That is why this article sits beside, not inside, [Phosphatidylserine Allergen Statement Checklist for US and EU Ingredient Buyers](/resources/phosphatidylserine-allergen-statement-us-eu/). The allergen statement answers the supplier-file question. This article answers the U.S. label-routing question.

### Match artwork to the specification, COA path, and customer questionnaire

The fifth step is the one procurement teams often skip. A technically legal label line can still be the wrong commercial label if it belongs to the wrong route. Before artwork approval, compare the label file to:

- the current specification
- the COA path or representative batch-document logic
- any customer or contract-manufacturer questionnaire already in circulation
- the route-specific support files already approved internally

This matters because the label may become the reference document downstream. If the label says sunflower phosphatidylserine while the approved item is still generic PS or a soy route, the artwork creates avoidable rework. If the customer questionnaire expects soy-linked allergen logic, but the label file removed the route wording, QA will have to reopen the file later.

## Common Mistakes in Phosphatidylserine Label Review

Most failures here are file-control failures, not design failures.

1. Leaving phosphatidylserine outside Supplement Facts as if it were optional marketing text.
2. Approving a generic PS label while procurement is still switching between soy and sunflower routes.
3. Repeating source wording randomly across Supplement Facts, ingredient list, and customer files without deciding which line is actually controlled.
4. Treating soy allergen disclosure as separate from the source decision.
5. Forgetting that an amount claim outside Supplement Facts still needs to be specific and consistent with the panel.
6. Letting final artwork move forward before QA has checked the label against the approved specification and downstream questionnaire.

The practical fix is simple: keep one question at the center of the file. **What exact phosphatidylserine route, amount-per-serving line, and allergen-routing logic is this U.S. label actually approving?**
