## The Short Answer Buyers Need First

If your team is reviewing phosphatidylserine for a California-facing supplement or nutrition project, the simplest workable Proposition 65 process is:

1. Lock the exact route first: general PS, soy PS, or sunflower PS.
2. Confirm whether the question is about supplier heavy-metals support, a customer questionnaire, or a finished-product California warning decision.
3. Review the current COA path, specification, and any available contaminant-support file together with the intended daily serving.
4. Keep the supplier file separate from the final Proposition 65 warning determination.
5. Record who owns the next answer: importer, contract manufacturer, brand, or legal/regulatory lead.

That is the practical distinction buyers often miss. Proposition 65 is not answered by the product name alone, and it is not answered by a generic statement that ignores exposure assumptions. OEHHA's lead page, for example, lists oral safe-harbor levels of **15 micrograms per day** for cancer and **0.5 micrograms per day** for reproductive toxicity. That does not make every PS project a lead-warning project. It shows why the California review depends on the listed chemical and the actual intake scenario being evaluated.

## Why Proposition 65 Review Is Its Own Buyer Task

### Proposition 65 is exposure-based, not ingredient-name-based

One reason teams mishandle Proposition 65 is that they treat it like a naming problem. They ask whether phosphatidylserine is "Prop 65 compliant" as if the answer sits inside the ingredient name or a generic supplier claim.

That is not how the official California framework works. OEHHA explains that a product does not require a warning simply because it contains a listed chemical; the question is whether the exposure triggers the law. The Proposition 65 list covers a wide range of chemicals, and some chemicals have established NSRLs or MADLs while others do not.

For phosphatidylserine buyers, the business lesson is straightforward:

| Buyer question | What the team is really trying to confirm |
| --- | --- |
| Do you have a Prop 65 statement? | Is there enough supplier support to evaluate a California exposure question for this route? |
| Is PS Prop 65 compliant? | Has anyone translated route data into the intended serving and listed-chemical framework? |
| Does a clean COA settle the issue? | No, because a COA alone does not decide the finished-product exposure analysis |
| Can sales promise no warning? | Not unless the responsible business has completed its California review |

That is why Proposition 65 review belongs beside, not inside, general specification review. [Phosphatidylserine COA, Specification, and Certificate Review](/resources/phosphatidylserine-coa-specification-certificate-review/) helps buyers compare document types. [Can US Manufacturers Rely on a PS Supplier COA?](/resources/phosphatidylserine-supplier-coa-qualification-us/) explains why a contract manufacturer may need more than a supplier batch report. Proposition 65 review answers a narrower question: **what supplier evidence exists, what exposure assumptions are being used, and who owns the California warning decision?**

### FDA supplement quality controls still do not answer the California warning question by themselves

Some buyers assume the California issue disappears if the ingredient already fits ordinary U.S. supplement quality controls. That is too broad.

FDA's dietary supplement CGMP framework in 21 CFR Part 111 requires quality control operations to ensure supplement quality, while FDA separately notes that toxic elements such as arsenic, lead, cadmium, and mercury matter in foods and dietary supplements. Those are important controls, but they do not automatically answer the Proposition 65 question, because California warning analysis is not identical to federal CGMP compliance.

## The Six-Part Workflow for Reviewing a PS Proposition 65 Request

### Lock the exact phosphatidylserine route and California sales path first

Before requesting any Proposition 65 support file, fix the commercial route in writing. Is the team comparing a general PS route for sourcing, or is it already quoting soy PS or sunflower PS? Will the finished product actually be sold in California, or is the question only being raised by a national distributor that wants one technical answer?

This matters because the California review becomes sloppy when the route and sales path are both moving. Without a stable route and market path, the review becomes a chain of email opinions instead of a controlled decision.

The opening note should usually record:

- exact product route
- intended finished daily serving or use assumption
- California sales expectation
- documents already in hand
- open Proposition 65 question still unresolved

### Separate supplier support files from the finished-product warning decision

The second step is to separate what the supplier can support from what the finished-product owner must decide.

A phosphatidylserine supplier may be able to provide:

- current specification
- current batch COA or representative COA style
- route identity and source information
- contaminant-support data or statement path, if available
- general commercial clarification on MOQ, packing, and product route

The supplier usually does **not** own every downstream California decision. The brand, contract manufacturer, or other responsible business still has to decide whether the finished intake scenario leads to a warning and whether additional testing or calculations are required.

That distinction also lines up with California's legal structure. In practical B2B terms, a raw-material supplier can be an important evidence source without becoming the only owner of the downstream California answer.

### Review COA data, specification language, and intended daily intake together

Once the file is assembled, buyers should review three things at the same time:

1. the exact phosphatidylserine route being quoted
2. the available ingredient-level contaminant or statement support
3. the finished-product intake assumption that will matter in California

| Checkpoint | Buyer question |
| --- | --- |
| Route match | Does the data belong to general PS, soy PS, or sunflower PS? |
| Data type | Is this a specification limit, a batch COA, a statement, or a separate support file? |
| Intake basis | What daily serving or finished-use assumption is the California review using? |
| Listed chemical focus | Is the question about lead, cadmium, another listed chemical, or a generic warning request? |
| Decision owner | Who will convert the data into a California warning decision? |

That table is why a simple "passes heavy metals" note is rarely enough. It may help supplier qualification, but the California review still needs a defined exposure basis.

### Map ownership across importer, contract manufacturer, and brand

The fourth step is to write down who owns which part of the answer. If nobody owns the California decision, sales will end up improvising it.

In a typical U.S. supplement project:

- the importer or procurement lead owns supplier collection
- QA reviews document completeness and consistency
- the contract manufacturer or brand owns formula-specific intake assumptions
- regulatory or legal review owns the final California warning position

That ownership split matters because Proposition 65 questions often appear at the moment a customer wants a yes-or-no answer.

### Escalate immediately when the listed-chemical or exposure basis is unclear

Escalation is appropriate when:

- the file does not identify which listed chemical is driving the California question
- the project has no stable daily serving assumption yet
- the only evidence in hand is a generic COA with no route-specific interpretation
- the customer wants a "no warning" promise before the brand or contract manufacturer has completed its own review
- the route changed after the original support files were collected

The escalation note does not need to be complicated. It should say which route is under review, what California question remains open, what intake assumption is being used or is still missing, and who owns the next decision.

### Close with a go, hold, or escalation decision

The final step is to record an outcome another team can use:

- **Go:** the route, supplier support, and California review ownership are aligned well enough for the current stage.
- **Hold:** the project may still be attractive, but the California question is not controlled enough yet.
- **Escalate:** the route needs additional testing, exposure review, or legal/regulatory sign-off before anyone makes a warning claim.

This closeout matters because Proposition 65 questions often resurface after sourcing is approved but before California sales or customer handoff.

## Common Mistakes in Phosphatidylserine Proposition 65 Review

Most failures here are workflow failures, not chemistry failures.

1. Asking whether phosphatidylserine is "Prop 65 compliant" before locking the exact route and finished-use assumption.
2. Treating a raw-material COA as if it automatically settles the California warning question.
3. Confusing federal supplement CGMP expectations with California warning analysis.
4. Letting sales promise "no warning required" before the responsible business has finished its exposure review.
5. Forgetting to identify which listed chemical, if any, is driving the request.
6. Reusing an old support file after the route, serving, or finished formula changed.

The practical fix is simple: keep one question at the center of the review. **What supplier evidence exists for this exact phosphatidylserine route, what intake assumption is being used, and who owns the California warning decision?**

## FAQ

### Does phosphatidylserine need a Proposition 65 statement?

Often buyers ask for one when a product may be sold in California, but the useful question is narrower: what supplier support exists for the exact route, and who owns the final warning decision for the finished product?

### Can a phosphatidylserine COA replace a Proposition 65 review?

No. A COA can be part of the evidence set, but Proposition 65 questions are usually about listed-chemical exposure under a defined intake scenario, not only about whether a raw material passed a general specification.

### Is Proposition 65 review the same as a heavy-metals specification review?

No. Heavy-metals review checks analytical data and specification limits. Proposition 65 review asks how those data relate to listed chemicals, intake assumptions, and warning responsibility in California.

### Who usually owns the final California warning decision?

Usually the finished-product owner, contract manufacturer, or brand regulatory/legal lead, not the raw-material supplier alone. The supplier supports the file, but the downstream business still owns its own exposure and warning analysis.
