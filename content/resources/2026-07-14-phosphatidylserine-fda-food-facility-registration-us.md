## Introduction

U.S. phosphatidylserine projects often slow down on a question that should be simple but rarely stays simple: **the supplier sent an FDA food facility registration file, so does that mean the product is approved for import and supplier qualification is finished?**

The short answer is **no**. ## The Short Answer Buyers Need First

If a phosphatidylserine supplier shares an FDA food facility registration file, buyers should read it as **one facility-level control document**, not as a complete U.S. approval packet.

The minimum practical workflow is:

1. Confirm that the registered party is the facility relevant to your quoted supply route.
2. Check that the registration is current enough for the commercial stage you are reviewing.
3. Keep the registration file separate from product-specific files such as the specification, source declaration, and COA path.
4. Identify which U.S. party owns FSVP or the modified dietary-supplement import responsibilities for that phosphatidylserine route.
5. Close the file with a clear go, hold, or escalation note instead of assuming the registration solves every downstream question.

That is the core buyer lesson. Registration matters, but it answers a narrower question than many teams assume.

## Why This U.S. Buyer Question Matters

### Registration is a facility requirement, not product approval

FDA's current food-facility registration materials say domestic and foreign food facilities engaged in manufacturing, processing, packing, or holding food for U.S. consumption generally must register with FDA unless an exemption applies. FDA also states that food facility registrations must be renewed every other year, and its small-entity guide says renewal runs from October 1 through December 31 of each even-numbered year.

Those are important control points, but they are still facility-level controls. FDA's own registration guidance says assignment of a registration number means only that the facility is registered. It does **not** convey FDA approval or endorsement of the facility or its products. FDA also states that it does not issue a certificate of registration or product status for food facilities, and that private certificates should not be treated as if FDA issued them.

For phosphatidylserine buyers, that distinction matters immediately. A registration file may support basic supplier legitimacy and import-file completeness, but it does not prove:

| Buyer question | What the registration can help show | What it does not prove by itself |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Is there a relevant food facility in the U.S. supply chain? | That a facility has registered with FDA where registration is required | That the quoted phosphatidylserine route is approved or appropriate for your use |
| Can this document support supplier screening? | That the facility-level file exists and can be reviewed | That the supplier is fully qualified in your own system |
| Is the product ready for purchase or release? | Very little by itself beyond facility-level context | Specification fit, COA logic, source control, batch readiness, or shipment release |
| Does the file replace other import records? | No | FSVP, prior notice, technical packet, or customer approval requirements |

This is why the most helpful internal sentence is simple: **food facility registration is a baseline facility document, not a phosphatidylserine product approval file.**

### Import review breaks when one file is asked to do every job

Most U.S. approval delays are not caused by the existence of the registration file. They happen because teams ask that file to cover work that belongs somewhere else.

Procurement may want to know whether the quoted supplier is real. QA may need source-specific technical files. Import teams may need clarity on who owns the foreign supplier verification program. Contract manufacturers may want to know whether incoming approval will depend on their own specification and identity-testing workflow. Customer-facing sales teams may only want a simple answer to "are you FDA registered?"

Those are different questions. When the same registration PDF is circulated as if it answers all of them, the approval packet starts to drift.

FDA's current food-facility registration page makes this easier to understand because it places registration next to other separate import-related systems and submissions, including advance notice. FDA's FSVP rule page makes the separation even clearer: importers must develop and follow an FSVP for each food and each foreign supplier, and the requirements for dietary supplements and dietary supplement components vary depending on the situation. In other words, the U.S. framework already treats facility registration, shipment notice, and importer verification as different controls. Buyers should do the same.

## The Five-Part Review Workflow

### Confirm who the registered party actually is

Start by answering a basic but often skipped question: **whose registration file is this?**

FDA's fee and registration FAQ states that traders and brokers are not required to register as food facilities under section 415. That means a buyer should not assume the commercial counterparty and the registered facility are automatically the same party. In phosphatidylserine sourcing, that distinction can matter if the quoted company is acting as an exporter, trading company, or brand-facing sales entity while the manufacturing, packing, or holding role sits elsewhere.

For a cleaner review, the file should tell you:

- legal entity on the registration-related document
- role in the supply chain
- whether that role matches the quoted phosphatidylserine route
- whether the registered facility is the one relevant to manufacturing, packing, or holding the product for U.S. consumption

If the file does not make that relationship clear, the buyer should hold the review until the commercial and facility roles are mapped correctly. This is especially important when reviewing [supplier onboarding](/resources/phosphatidylserine-supplier-onboarding/) or building a downstream [distributor document pack](/resources/phosphatidylserine-distributor-document-pack/). Those workflows move more smoothly when the facility identity is settled early.

### Check whether the registration is current and commercially relevant

Once the party is clear, the next question is whether the registration status is current enough for the transaction stage.

FDA states that required food-facility registrations must be renewed every other year. That means a buyer should not treat an old registration reference as permanent proof of current status. A file that was acceptable in one review cycle may need refresh or reconfirmation in the next, especially if the project is moving from exploratory sourcing to a first commercial order.

This step is not only about dates. It is about commercial relevance. Buyers should ask:

- Is the registration file current enough for the order stage we are in?
- Does it appear tied to the same facility role we are relying on now?
- Are we looking at a supplier-provided reference file, a current registration copy, or only a screenshot or private certificate?
- Has the commercial route changed since the file first entered the approval packet?

The strongest answer is not usually a long dossier. It is a clear statement that the facility-level registration reference is current for the stage under review, together with a refreshed technical file set for the exact phosphatidylserine route.

### Separate registration from product-specific technical files

This is the step that prevents the most confusion. A facility registration file should sit beside the technical packet, not inside it as a substitute.

For phosphatidylserine, the product-specific files still need to answer separate questions:

| Product-specific file | What it should answer |
| --- | --- |
| Current specification | What exact route, grade, and technical parameters are being quoted |
| COA path or current batch COA | How batch-linked evidence will be reviewed at the right stage |
| Source statement or declaration | Whether the route is general PS, soy PS, or sunflower PS and how it should be handled internally |
| Packaging and storage references | What the buyer should expect commercially and operationally |
| Market-specific support notes | What destination-market review points still need to be handled downstream |

The registration file does none of that by itself.

That is why U.S. teams should avoid replying to customer or internal QA questions with only "the factory is FDA registered." A better response is: the supplier has a food-facility registration reference, and we are separately reviewing the current specification, COA path, source route, and import responsibilities for the quoted phosphatidylserine item.

It answers the narrower supplier-registration question before incoming release or Part 111 qualification logic takes over.

### Identify who owns FSVP or modified dietary-supplement import responsibilities

Once the registration file is separated properly, the importer still needs to identify who owns the U.S. verification responsibilities for the food being imported.

FDA's FSVP final-rule summary says importers must develop, maintain, and follow an FSVP for each food and each foreign supplier. The same FDA page also explains that the requirements for dietary supplements vary, and that importers who establish and verify compliance with certain specifications for dietary supplement components and packaging under the dietary-supplement CGMP rule may fall under modified requirements instead of most standard FSVP requirements.

For phosphatidylserine buyers, the practical point is not to force one universal answer. It is to name the responsible party and route clearly. The review file should identify:

- who the U.S. importer is for the food
- whether the project is relying on standard FSVP or a modified dietary-supplement path
- who is establishing and verifying the relevant specifications
- whether the contract manufacturer, importer, or downstream customer controls the next review stage

This is one of the cleanest ways to keep facility registration in its proper lane. Registration helps identify a facility context. FSVP or modified dietary-supplement responsibilities tell you who is responsible for the importer-side verification framework. Those are connected, but not interchangeable.

### Close the file with a go, hold, or escalation decision

The final step is to close the review with a short decision note that others can use.

Use one of these outcomes:

- **Go:** the registration file is current enough, the relevant party is clear, and the rest of the technical packet can move forward.
- **Hold:** the registration file exists, but the responsible party, renewal status, or facility relevance is still unclear.
- **Escalate:** the commercial route changed, the document source is weak, or the importer-side FSVP ownership is not defined well enough to proceed.

This matters because registration questions tend to return later if they are not closed early. A distributor may ask for it again. A contract manufacturer may ask whether it changes incoming approval. A customer may confuse it with product authorization. The best defense is a short, explicit closeout note explaining what the file does prove and what other files remain necessary.

## FAQ

### Does FDA food facility registration mean a phosphatidylserine supplier is approved by FDA?

No. FDA's food-facility registration guidance says assignment of a registration number means the facility is registered, not that FDA has approved or endorsed the facility or its products.

### Is an FDA food facility registration certificate enough for a U.S. phosphatidylserine import file?

No. Buyers should still review the current specification, COA path, source route, packaging context, and the U.S. party's importer-verification responsibilities for the exact phosphatidylserine item being sourced.

### Do traders or brokers need to be the same party as the registered food facility?

Not necessarily. FDA states that traders and brokers are not required to register as food facilities under section 415, so buyers should confirm whether the commercial counterparty and the relevant registered facility are the same or different parties.

### How often should buyers reconfirm a food facility registration reference?

At minimum, buyers should reconfirm it whenever the commercial route changes, the order is moving into a new approval stage, or the review cycle crosses into a new biennial registration period. The buyer should not assume an old registration file is current forever.

### Does food facility registration replace FSVP for imported phosphatidylserine?

No. FDA treats these as separate controls. Importers must maintain an FSVP for each food and each foreign supplier, although certain dietary-supplement situations may use modified requirements instead of most standard FSVP requirements.
