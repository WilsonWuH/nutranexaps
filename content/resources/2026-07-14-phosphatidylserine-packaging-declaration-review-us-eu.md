## Why Packaging Declaration Review Is Its Own Buyer Task

### EU buyers must separate food-contact packaging compliance from ingredient authorization

Europe-facing ingredient teams often collect legal and technical files in one folder, which creates a false sense that every document answers the same question. It does not. The EU novel-food framework answers one question. A phosphatidylserine specification answers another. A packaging declaration answers a different one again: whether the packaging material intended to contact the ingredient is supported under the relevant food-contact rules and manufacturing controls.

The Commission's food contact materials pages make this distinction clear. Food contact materials include packaging and containers. The framework regulation requires that they not release constituents into food at harmful levels or change food composition, taste, or odour in an unacceptable way. The legislation page also notes that the framework provides for compliance documentation and traceability, while the GMP regulation requires documented quality assurance and quality control systems plus suitable starting materials for the manufacturing process.

For phosphatidylserine buyers, that means a Europe-ready approval file should separate at least four questions:

| Buyer question | File that helps answer it | What it does not prove by itself |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Is the ingredient route commercially usable in Europe? | Route-specific regulatory and product file | Whether the liner or contact layer is covered properly |
| Is the product technically acceptable? | Specification and COA path | Whether the packaging material meets food-contact expectations |
| Is the packing configuration operationally workable? | Quote, MOQ, drum count, dispatch file | Whether the contact layer has the right declaration scope |
| Is the contact packaging supported? | Packaging declaration or controlled support file | Whether the ingredient itself is authorized for the intended market |

This is why a packaging declaration review belongs in its own lane. It sits downstream of broad ingredient positioning, but upstream of import receipt, warehouse release, or customer technical handoff.

### US buyers should confirm intended use and transport responsibility, not just a generic food-grade claim

The U.S. side creates a different failure pattern. Buyers often hear simple phrases such as "food-grade liner," "FDA compliant bag," or "packaging suitable for food ingredients" and treat them as enough. FDA's own food-contact materials guidance points to a more disciplined standard.

FDA says food contact substances are used in materials for manufacturing, packing, packaging, transporting, or holding food. It also says manufacturers must ensure that food contact substances comply with the specifications and limitations in applicable authorizations. FDA's food contact notification guidance further explains that authorizations are tied to the manufacturer, the substance, and the conditions of use, not just to a broad product category. FDA's regulatory-status page also notes that buyers may ask the manufacturer for a letter of guaranty certifying that a particular product is acceptable for the intended food-contact use.

That means U.S. buyers should avoid treating packaging review as a marketing statement. The stronger question is: **does the supporting file match the actual intended packaging use for this phosphatidylserine route and shipment configuration?**

Transport review belongs in the same conversation. FDA's sanitary transportation rule covers shippers, receivers, loaders, and carriers in covered U.S. food transport operations and sets requirements for equipment, operations, training, and records. For Europe, transport hygiene rules under Regulation (EC) No 852/2004 require food conveyances and containers to be kept clean, maintained in good repair, and used in a way that protects food from contamination. The commercial lesson is straightforward: packaging review is incomplete if nobody has defined which party controls condition, cleanliness, and documentation during transit and receipt.

## The Six-Part Workflow for Reviewing PS Packaging Declarations and Transport Records

### Lock the exact packaging configuration and commercial route first

Before requesting any packaging document, write down what shipment setup is actually under review. Is the product general PS, soy PS, or sunflower PS? Is the shipment using the standard bulk drum route? Is the buyer reviewing one-drum qualification material, a first commercial lot, or a distributor handoff file? Is the concern the inner liner, the primary drum-contact layer, palletized wrapping, or transport condition control?

This sounds basic, but many packaging reviews fail because one team is asking a regulatory question while another is looking at a commercial photo from a different configuration. The minimum route note should usually contain:

- exact phosphatidylserine route
- current packing format under quotation
- commercial stage
- destination market or markets
- which packaging component is being reviewed

Once that note exists, procurement, QA, warehousing, and downstream customers are at least talking about the same item.

### Separate food-contact compliance from commercial packaging details

The second step is to stop mixing commercial packing facts with compliance support.

For example, Nutranexa's current site gives a verified baseline of **25 kg MOQ** and **25 kg net per drum** for phosphatidylserine. That is useful and should be part of the operational review. But it does not answer the separate question of what declaration or support file covers the contact material or how the file should be interpreted for the destination market.

The clean review structure usually looks like this:

| File type | Main purpose |
| --- | --- |
| Quote or item sheet | Drum count, MOQ, shipment planning, order-stage assumptions |
| Packing photo or dispatch proof | Physical configuration and operational context |
| Packaging declaration or support file | Contact-material scope and document traceability |
| Specification and COA path | Product identity and analytical approval |
| Receiving or transport file | Condition control and handoff responsibility |

This structure keeps one common buyer mistake from recurring: treating a packaging photo as if it were evidence of regulatory coverage.

### Check whether the declaration actually covers the contact layer in use

Once the document types are separated, the buyer should ask a narrower question: **what exactly does this declaration cover?**

On the EU side, the legislation page explains that plastic materials have specific rules under Regulation (EU) No 10/2011 and that the broader framework includes compliance documentation and traceability. That means buyers should not assume one declaration automatically covers every layer, every configuration, or every commercial use pattern. On the U.S. side, FDA's food-contact materials resources make the same point indirectly by focusing on intended use, conditions of use, and manufacturer-specific scope.

A practical buyer checklist is:

- Does the file identify the relevant packaging material or contact layer clearly enough?
- Is the file current enough for the commercial stage?
- Does the supporting statement appear tied to the supplier or manufacturer actually responsible for that material?
- Is the intended use broad enough for the actual phosphatidylserine bulk packaging route being supplied?
- Is the file being used as a declaration, a guaranty, a regulatory reference, or just a marketing attachment?

If the answer to any of those questions is vague, the file should be held rather than assumed forward.

### Confirm the US authorization path or guaranty support for the intended packaging use

U.S. buyers often want one simple statement that they can drop into the approval folder. FDA's own pages suggest a more precise approach.

FDA maintains public inventories for food contact substances and explains that authorizations can include the manufacturer, intended use, limitations, specifications, and effective date. Its regulatory-status guidance says it is the manufacturer's responsibility to ensure compliance with applicable authorizations and notes that a buyer may request a letter of guaranty for the intended use.

For phosphatidylserine buyers, the practical takeaway is not that every packaging file must include an FDA citation on its face. It is that the file logic should be strong enough to answer the downstream question without overclaiming. A sound U.S. review typically asks:

1. Who is responsible for the packaging material support file?
2. Is the claimed food-contact path tied to the actual intended use, not just a broad category claim?
3. Does the buyer need a guaranty-style statement, a supplier declaration, or a more specific support path for its internal review?
4. Is anyone treating a packaging support file as if FDA directly approved the finished phosphatidylserine package? If so, stop and rewrite the internal note.

This step protects buyers from the same type of confusion seen in facility-registration review: one true fact gets overstretched into a claim it was never meant to support.

### Review transport-condition and hygiene responsibilities before shipment release

A packaging declaration by itself does not control what happens in transit. Buyers should therefore decide who owns condition control before the drums move.

For Europe, transport hygiene rules require conveyances and containers used for foodstuffs to be kept clean, maintained in good repair and condition, and used in a way that protects food from contamination. For covered U.S. transport operations, FDA's sanitary transportation rule emphasizes equipment design and maintenance, transportation operations, training, and records.

That means a cross-border phosphatidylserine review should answer these questions before release:

| Transport checkpoint | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| Who owns sanitary condition instructions? | Prevents the shipper, carrier, and receiver from assuming someone else is responsible |
| What condition controls are relevant to this route? | Keeps the file tied to the real product and packaging setup |
| What records exist for handoff and receipt? | Supports warehouse and QA closeout instead of relying on memory |
| Is the transport review separate from the COA review? | Avoids confusing analytical approval with logistics control |

Those pages help with dispatch and receipt execution. The article you are reading sits one step earlier, where the packaging and transport-document logic is still being defined.

### Close the file with a go, hold, or escalation decision

The last step is to turn the review into a usable decision.

- **Go:** the exact packaging configuration is clear, the support file covers the relevant contact layer or intended use well enough for the stage, and transport responsibilities are assigned.
- **Hold:** the commercial setup is clear, but the declaration scope or support logic is still incomplete.
- **Escalate:** the packaging route changed, the file owner is unclear, or the buyer needs additional regulatory or customer review before approval.

This matters because packaging questions repeat. Distributors ask again. Contract manufacturers ask again. Warehouses ask again when the first lot arrives. The only way to stop the file from drifting is to close it with a short note that states what was reviewed, what was not, and what the downstream team may still need.

## Common Mistakes in Phosphatidylserine Packaging Review

Most failures here are document-control failures, not material failures.

1. Treating MOQ and drum size as if they answer food-contact packaging questions.
2. Accepting a generic food-grade claim without checking who issued it, what it covers, and which intended use it supports.
3. Reusing one declaration across changed packaging configurations or source routes.
4. Forgetting that transport-condition responsibility is part of shipment readiness, not an afterthought.
5. Letting packaging review get mixed into the product COA or novel-food file until no one can tell which question was actually answered.

The practical fix is simple: keep one packaging question open until it is really closed. **What controlled file supports this exact contact packaging and transport setup for this exact phosphatidylserine shipment stage?**

## FAQ

### Is a drum photo enough to approve phosphatidylserine packaging for import?

No. A drum photo can help confirm the commercial packing format, but buyers should still review the packaging support file or declaration logic, the contact-layer scope, and the transport-condition responsibilities for the exact shipment stage.

### Does a food-grade claim automatically prove the liner is acceptable in both the EU and the US?

No. Buyers should confirm what the claim actually covers, who issued it, and whether it supports the intended packaging use and destination-market workflow being reviewed.

### Should packaging review stay separate from phosphatidylserine novel-food or product-specification review?

Yes. Those files answer different questions. Product and market authorization logic should not be used as a substitute for packaging-contact or transport-document review.

### What should U.S. buyers ask for if the packaging support file is vague?

They should ask for a clearer support path tied to the intended use, such as a supplier statement, guaranty-style support where appropriate, or a better defined regulatory-reference trail for the packaging material under review.

### Why do transport records matter if the packaging declaration is already acceptable?

Because packaging support does not control what happens during transit. Buyers still need clarity on hygiene, condition control, handoff, and records before shipment release and receipt.
