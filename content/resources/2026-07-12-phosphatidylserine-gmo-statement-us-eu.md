## Why GMO Statement Review Is a Separate Buyer Task

### US finished-food disclosure rules and supplier statements are not the same thing

US buyers often confuse three different questions.

1. Does the supplier have a current GMO statement for the ingredient route being quoted?
2. Does the buyer have enough records for its internal customer questionnaire or procurement file?
3. Does the final finished product trigger any separate US disclosure or labeling assessment?

Those questions overlap, but they are not identical. USDA AMS says the National Bioengineered Food Disclosure Standard requires food manufacturers, importers, and certain retailers to ensure bioengineered foods are appropriately disclosed, and it defines bioengineered foods as foods containing detectable modified genetic material of the type described in the rule. AMS also maintains the current list of bioengineered foods in 7 CFR 66.6, which includes soybean. That is one reason soy-route phosphatidylserine projects often trigger extra questionnaire and recordkeeping questions in US customer files.

But a supplier GMO statement is still not the same as a finished-label decision. A buyer may request a statement because a contract manufacturer, private-label customer, or distributor needs a controlled source declaration in the technical file long before retail labeling is finalized. FDA's guidance on voluntary labeling reinforces that statements about whether ingredients were or were not produced using genetic engineering should be truthful and not misleading. For B2B teams, that means a short commercial assurance is weaker than a current controlled declaration tied to the exact route under review.

### EU traceability and labeling rules make document control more important

EU buyers usually reach the issue from a different angle. Regulation (EC) No 1829/2003 lays down the framework for genetically modified food and feed, while Regulation (EC) No 1830/2003 addresses traceability and labeling of GMOs and the traceability of food and feed products produced from GMOs. Those regulations also include the familiar 0.9 percent threshold logic for adventitious or technically unavoidable presence in certain contexts, but that threshold does not remove the buyer's need for a clean source-specific document workflow.

In practical B2B terms, the EU lesson is not "every phosphatidylserine file needs the same GMO wording." The lesson is that traceability and labeling questions should be supported by controlled records. If a distributor, importer, or finished-brand customer asks how the route was documented, QA should be able to point to a current declaration rather than reconstructing the answer from scattered emails.

That is why GMO review becomes its own buyer task. It sits between source identity, customer-questionnaire support, and later label or dossier review. If teams leave it undefined, the project slows down later when the commercial path is already moving.

## The Six-Part Workflow for Reviewing a PS GMO Statement

### Lock the exact phosphatidylserine route and review purpose first

Before requesting any GMO file, fix the route and the question in writing. Is the team reviewing general phosphatidylserine while comparing sources, a soy-specific route for an established formula, or a sunflower route for a non-soy customer brief? Is the file for importer approval, contract-manufacturer onboarding, a distributor questionnaire, or a finished-brand technical pack?

Without that first step, the buyer collects documents that cannot be interpreted cleanly. A procurement team may ask for a "non-GMO statement" while QA is still comparing soy and sunflower routes. By the time the customer asks for a formal declaration, nobody is sure which route the statement was supposed to cover.

The route note should usually contain:

- product route
- market scope
- internal item or quotation reference
- review purpose
- responsible QA or regulatory owner

That one control note keeps the GMO question attached to the actual buying decision instead of to a vague product family.

### Request a current source-specific GMO statement instead of a casual assurance

The next step is to ask for the right file in the right form. Buyers often ask, "Is this non-GMO?" and receive a quick answer that is commercially useful but not controlled enough for internal approval. A better process is to request a current GMO statement or GMO declaration tied to the exact phosphatidylserine route being quoted.

The table below separates what buyers should be looking for.

| File element | What it helps answer | What it does not prove by itself |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Source-specific GMO statement | How the supplier is documenting the exact route under review | Whether the buyer's final finished product can make a specific market claim |
| Specification | Which route, source wording, and technical identity are being quoted | Whether a customer questionnaire is fully answered |
| COA | How one batch or reference batch is reported analytically | Whether GMO status was formally declared for the route |
| Sales email note | That the supplier addressed the question commercially | Whether QA has a current controlled file for approval |

This is especially important for source-specific routes. A soy-route project often triggers more immediate questionnaire pressure because soybean appears on the USDA bioengineered foods list and because customer review teams may ask follow-up questions automatically. A sunflower route can still require a statement, but buyers should not treat the route name alone as the declaration.

### Separate supplier GMO files from finished-product disclosure and label decisions

Once the file arrives, keep the buyer workflow disciplined. A supplier GMO statement is an upstream control document. It helps the buyer's technical, regulatory, and customer-service teams understand how the supplier is documenting the ingredient route. It does not replace the buyer's own finished-label decision for a supplement, functional food, or other downstream product.

That distinction matters in both markets.

- In the United States, the supplier's statement may support questionnaire handling, sourcing review, and recordkeeping, while the brand or manufacturer still owns its finished-product BE disclosure assessment.
- In the EU, the supplier's statement may support traceability, sourcing review, and customer communication, while the importer, brand owner, or regulatory team still owns the final market-facing review.

Keeping those steps separate prevents overclaiming. It stops the team from converting one supplier statement into unsupported packaging language or a broad marketing claim that no one has approved.

### Check the statement against the specification, source route, and customer file

The strongest review is comparative, not isolated. Buyers should compare the GMO statement against the rest of the commercial and technical packet.

Practical checks include:

1. Does the product route on the statement match the route on the specification?
2. Does the source wording match the quotation and customer file?
3. Is the statement current enough for the approval stage, or is it clearly an outdated reference?
4. If the buyer is serving both US and EU customers, has the team recorded how the same file will be used in each workflow?
5. Is the file stored with the same route-specific technical pack as the COA, source declaration, and specification?

### Handle soy-route and sunflower-route questions without assumptions

This is where many teams make avoidable errors. They assume soy route means one answer, sunflower route means another answer, and no further document work is needed.

The better approach is more precise.

| Route under review | Typical buyer question | Better control action |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Soy phosphatidylserine | Does the customer need a current GMO declaration because the route is soy-based? | Request a current soy-route statement and attach it to the route-specific file |
| Sunflower phosphatidylserine | Can we rely on the route name alone for non-GMO or questionnaire handling? | Request a current sunflower-route statement rather than inferring status |
| General PS comparison stage | Can one statement cover all future route options? | No. Fix the route first, then request the route-specific statement |

The key lesson is that source identity and document identity should move together. Soy-route review may attract more immediate scrutiny because soybean is explicitly addressed in the US bioengineered-foods framework and is frequently called out in customer questionnaires. Sunflower-route review may look simpler, but it still needs a controlled file if the buyer will use the statement in procurement approval, resale support, or customer documentation.

### Close with a market-specific go, hold, or escalation note

The last step is to close the file explicitly.

- **Go:** the buyer has a current route-specific statement and enough supporting alignment to proceed.
- **Hold:** the project is commercially active, but the route-specific statement is missing, outdated, or mismatched.
- **Escalate:** the route changed, the supplier statement conflicts with the file set, or the customer is asking for a deeper market-specific review.

This closeout matters because many teams finish the technical conversation but never record the commercial outcome. Procurement thinks the file is approved. QA thinks it is pending. Regulatory assumes a customer-specific claim review still needs to happen. That ambiguity creates more delay than the document request itself.

## Where Phosphatidylserine GMO Reviews Usually Break Down

The most common problems are procedural.

1. Asking whether the material is non-GMO instead of requesting a current route-specific statement.
2. Letting procurement quote one route while QA reviews a declaration for another.
3. Treating a COA, product nickname, or allergen statement as if it replaces a GMO declaration.
4. Using a supplier statement directly as finished-label language without internal review.
5. Reusing an old declaration after the route, customer, or market changed.

These errors force the team to rebuild the source-document logic after the technical pack has already moved into customer or manufacturing review.

## FAQ

### Can a phosphatidylserine COA replace a GMO statement?

No. A COA can support batch review and analytical comparison, but it is not the same as a route-specific GMO declaration. Buyers should request the GMO statement as its own controlled file.

### Does soy phosphatidylserine always need more GMO review than sunflower phosphatidylserine?

Soy-route projects often trigger more questionnaire attention because soybean is part of the US bioengineered-foods framework and is commonly called out in customer reviews. But sunflower-route projects should still use a current route-specific statement rather than relying on assumptions.

### Should importers and contract manufacturers use the same GMO statement?

They should use the same current controlled route-specific declaration, even if each party stores it in a different approval packet. The important point is that procurement, QA, and manufacturing are not working from conflicting source assumptions.

### What is the first file I should request if my quotation only says PS powder?

Request the current specification and route confirmation first. Once the route is fixed, ask for the route-specific GMO statement, the COA support path, and any related customer-facing support files you need for the same item.

### Can a supplier GMO statement be copied directly onto a finished label?

Not automatically. The supplier file is an upstream technical document. Final finished-label or disclosure wording should still go through the buyer's own regulatory, legal, and artwork review for the target market.
