## Introduction

An imported phosphatidylserine shipment can have a commercial importer, customs broker, manufacturer, distributor, and brand involved at the same time. That does not make them interchangeable under the Foreign Supplier Verification Programs rule. The FSVP file must identify the covered importer, food, and foreign supplier and show how hazards and supplier performance are evaluated and verified.

The direct answer is: **determine FSVP scope before the purchase order and entry filing, not when the shipment reaches the port.** Confirm who is the U.S. owner or consignee at entry, or the properly designated U.S. agent or representative when there is no U.S. owner or consignee. Then create a written, food-and-supplier-specific program covering hazard analysis, supplier evaluation and approval, appropriate verification, corrective action, reevaluation, qualified individuals, and retrievable records.

This guide addresses imported [phosphatidylserine](/products/phosphatidylserine/), including [soy PS](/products/soy-phosphatidylserine/) and [sunflower PS](/products/sunflower-phosphatidylserine/). It is operational guidance, not legal advice. Exemptions, modified requirements, dietary-supplement provisions, and the exact identity of the FSVP importer should be confirmed for the transaction.

## The Short Answer: One Entry Needs Clear Ownership

FDA’s FSVP rule generally requires an importer to develop, maintain, and follow an FSVP for each food and foreign supplier, unless an exemption or modified requirement applies. FDA defines the FSVP importer as the U.S. owner or consignee of the food at entry. If there is no U.S. owner or consignee, it is the U.S. agent or representative of the foreign owner or consignee, confirmed in a signed statement of consent.

The customs broker can transmit entry data, but broker activity does not automatically transfer the importer’s verification duties. Before shipment, the commercial team should put one name against each role:

- FSVP importer
- importer of record
- U.S. owner or consignee
- foreign supplier
- foreign manufacturer or processor
- customs broker
- receiving facility or contract manufacturer
- regulatory and quality record owner

One company may fill several roles, but the file should never rely on assumptions.

## 1. Decide Whether and How FSVP Applies

### Identify the exact imported food

Do not scope the file as “PS” only. Record:

- soy, sunflower, or other confirmed source
- supplier product name and code
- composition, carrier, or matrix where applicable
- intended use as a dietary ingredient or conventional-food ingredient
- packaging, lot format, and storage conditions
- foreign manufacturing or processing site

A source, composition, or site change can affect the hazard analysis and supplier evaluation. Treat a materially different product as a review trigger rather than an administrative rename.

### Check exemptions and modified requirements

The FSVP regulation includes exemptions and modified provisions for defined circumstances. Dietary supplements and components intended for use in dietary supplements have specific treatment depending on who imports them and which Part 111 obligations apply. Do not conclude “dietary supplement ingredient equals exempt” from a sales description.

Have a qualified reviewer document:

- the regulatory category at import
- the importer’s role
- whether a full FSVP, modified requirement, or exemption applies
- the specific legal basis and evidence retained
- what requirements remain even when a modification applies

If the same PS is imported for both supplement and conventional-food customers, do not assume one scope conclusion covers both routes.

## 2. Assign Qualified Individuals and Record Ownership

FSVP activities must be performed by qualified individuals with the education, training, or experience needed for the activity and the ability to read and understand the language of the records reviewed.

Assign named owners for:

- applicability and importer determination
- hazard analysis
- foreign supplier performance evaluation
- verification planning and review
- entry-data maintenance
- corrective action
- reevaluation
- record retrieval during an FDA request

Translation is a control point. A bilingual supplier document can help, but the U.S. reviewer must be able to understand the evidence used. Store controlled English translations when needed and keep them connected to the source document and version.

## 3. Perform a Food-Specific Hazard Analysis

FDA’s rule requires evaluation of known or reasonably foreseeable biological, chemical, and physical hazards and a determination of whether they require a control. The analysis should consider the PS product, source materials, processing, packaging, storage, and intended use.

Potential review categories may include:

- biological hazards associated with the material or process
- chemical contaminants, residues, allergens, or undeclared source material
- economically motivated substitution where it could affect safety
- physical hazards
- hazards introduced or controlled by the foreign supplier
- hazards controlled later by the importer’s customer or another entity

This is not a fixed Nutranexa hazard list. The qualified individual must use product-specific information and applicable U.S. requirements. A [COA review](/resources/phosphatidylserine-coa-specification-certificate-review/) can supply evidence, but the COA does not replace the hazard analysis.

The analysis should identify:

- hazard
- cause or source
- severity and probability basis
- whether a control is required
- who controls it
- verification evidence
- review date and qualified individual

## 4. Evaluate the Foreign Supplier

Supplier evaluation combines the risk posed by the food with the supplier’s performance. FDA identifies factors such as applicable food-safety regulations, compliance information, food-safety history, responsiveness in correcting problems, storage and transportation practices, and other relevant factors.

Build a supplier evaluation file that may include:

- legal entity and site identity
- product and source route
- process description sufficient for hazard review
- applicable FDA registration information where required
- inspection, warning-letter, import-alert, recall, and compliance searches
- audit reports relevant to the product and controls
- test history and trend review
- complaint, deviation, and corrective-action history
- change-notification process
- storage and transport controls

Nutranexa’s website provides manufacturing, quality, packaging, dispatch, specification, and COA evidence paths. Those materials can support evaluation. The U.S. importer still owns its evaluation and should verify current, product-applicable records.

## 5. Approve the Supplier and Select Verification Activities

Written procedures should ensure import from approved foreign suppliers or, when necessary and appropriate, temporary use of an unapproved supplier under adequate pre-import verification.

Verification options can include:

- on-site audit
- sampling and testing
- review of relevant food-safety records
- another appropriate activity supported by the risk evaluation

The choice and frequency must fit the hazard and supplier evaluation. An annual audit is not automatically required for every PS import, while a certificate alone is not automatically sufficient. When a hazard controlled by the foreign supplier has the serious adverse health consequences or death to humans or animals characteristic described in the rule, audit expectations require particular attention unless another approach is appropriately justified and documented.

For each activity, state:

- purpose and hazard addressed
- who performs it
- frequency or trigger
- acceptance criteria
- records reviewed
- reviewer conclusion
- action when evidence is late, incomplete, or adverse

If another entity performs an evaluation or verification activity, the importer must still review and assess the relevant documentation as permitted by the rule. Do not simply file a third-party certificate.

## 6. Connect Supplier Documents to the Imported Lot

The FSVP master file and shipment file should connect:

**approved supplier → approved product → current specification → batch COA and other planned verification → PO → entry → receiving lot**

For a shipment, verify:

- supplier and foreign site match the approved record
- product code, source, and composition match
- specification revision is current
- lot and packaging details are consistent across invoice, packing list, COA, and labels
- required verification evidence is complete
- deviations or changes have been reviewed
- FSVP importer entry data are correct

Keep the [Prior Notice workflow](/resources/phosphatidylserine-prior-notice-checklist-us/) and [food-facility registration review](/resources/phosphatidylserine-fda-food-facility-registration-us/) separate. They interact with import readiness but do not replace FSVP.

## 7. Prepare Entry Data and Record Retrieval

At entry, the FSVP importer’s name, electronic mailing address, and FDA-recognized unique facility identifier must be provided as required. FDA currently recognizes the DUNS number for this purpose; verify current FDA instructions before filing.

Create a controlled broker instruction that identifies:

- exact FSVP importer legal name
- electronic mailing address
- recognized UFI
- product and supplier mapping
- contact for discrepancies
- prohibition on substituting another entity without approval

Test record retrieval before the first shipment. The responsible team should be able to produce the program, hazard analysis, supplier evaluation, verification records, corrective actions, and reevaluation evidence promptly in English or with appropriate translation.

## 8. Correct, Reevaluate, and Control Change

Take appropriate corrective action when the supplier does not produce the food consistent with applicable requirements. The response may include investigating, stopping use, changing verification, rejecting or holding material, or discontinuing the supplier until the issue is resolved.

FDA’s overview states that food and supplier performance should be reevaluated at least every three years or sooner when new information becomes known. Change triggers can include:

- new hazard or regulatory information
- source crop, carrier, process, or site change
- adverse test trend
- audit or inspection finding
- warning letter, import alert, recall, or significant complaint
- repeated document mismatch
- transport or storage failure

Date the review and record why the existing verification remains suitable or how it changed.

## Common FSVP Mistakes

1. Assigning the customs broker as FSVP importer by default.
2. Using one generic program for multiple foods and suppliers.
3. Treating dietary-ingredient status as an automatic exemption.
4. Filing a hazard-analysis certificate without performing the importer’s analysis.
5. Selecting annual testing or audits without linking them to hazards.
6. Relying on third-party work without reviewing and assessing it.
7. Allowing product, source, or site changes to bypass reevaluation.
8. Mixing FSVP, Prior Notice, registration, and importer-of-record duties into one unchecked label.
9. Discovering missing English records only after an FDA request.

## FAQ

### Is the customs broker automatically the FSVP importer?

No. The FSVP importer is generally the U.S. owner or consignee at entry, or the properly designated U.S. agent or representative when there is no U.S. owner or consignee. A broker may transmit data without owning the program.

### Does one FSVP cover soy and sunflower PS from the same supplier?

Not automatically. Source, composition, process, hazards, and intended use can differ. The importer should define each food and foreign supplier combination and document whether a shared element is justified.

### Can a supplier COA satisfy FSVP?

A COA can be part of a verification activity when appropriate, but it does not by itself determine applicability, perform the hazard analysis, evaluate supplier performance, approve the supplier, or address every hazard.

### Are imported dietary ingredients exempt from FSVP?

Some dietary-supplement and component circumstances have exemptions or modified requirements, but the conclusion depends on the importer and applicable Part 111 controls. Document the exact regulatory basis rather than relying on the ingredient name.

### How often must the supplier be reevaluated?

FDA describes reevaluation at least every three years and sooner when new information about hazards or supplier performance becomes known. Verification activities may have different, risk-based frequencies.

## Conclusion

FSVP readiness is an ownership and evidence system, not one certificate. Identify the importer and product before entry, perform a qualified hazard and supplier review, select verification that addresses the actual risk, connect evidence to each shipment, and control change.

When those elements are clear before the PO, customs filing becomes the last data handoff rather than the first time anyone asks who owns imported-food verification.
