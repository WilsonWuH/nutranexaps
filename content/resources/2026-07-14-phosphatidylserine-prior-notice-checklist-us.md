U.S. phosphatidylserine projects often move smoothly through quotation, specification review, and supplier screening until a more operational question appears: **what exactly must be ready before the shipment goes into FDA Prior Notice and broker entry filing?**

The short commercial answer is that buyers should prepare **one controlled shipment-data file for the exact phosphatidylserine route being imported**, then hand that file to the broker or internal filer before the prior-notice clock starts running. ## The Short Answer Buyers Need First

If your team is importing phosphatidylserine into the United States, the cleanest prior-notice workflow is:

1. Fix the exact product route, ship-from route, mode of transport, and arrival plan first.
2. Decide who is submitting the prior notice and whether the filing will go through ACE or FDA's Prior Notice System Interface.
3. Prepare one shipment-data sheet that matches the same commercial and technical file everyone else is using.
4. Keep prior notice separate from supplier approval, registration, FSVP, and lot-release decisions.
5. Refile if key information changes after FDA confirms the notice for review.

That is the practical buyer lesson. Prior notice is an import-arrival control. It helps FDA review the incoming food shipment. It does **not** prove the supplier is approved, the batch is released, or the full technical packet is complete.

## Why Prior Notice Is Its Own Buyer Task

### Prior notice is arrival notice, not supplier approval

FDA's prior-notice overview explains that FDA must receive advance notification of food imported or offered for import into the United States, and that the purpose is to support more effective targeting of import inspections and help protect the food supply. That makes prior notice important, but it also makes its purpose narrower than many buyers assume.

For phosphatidylserine teams, the distinction looks like this:

| Buyer question | Control or file that helps answer it | What prior notice does not prove by itself |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Is FDA receiving advance notice of this shipment? | Prior notice filing and confirmation logic | That the supplier is fully approved in your own system |
| Is the facility or commercial party acceptable? | Supplier qualification and commercial review | That the product route, documents, and batch are approved |
| Is the imported ingredient technically usable? | Current specification, COA path, and internal QA review | That the shipment can be released into production |
| Who owns importer-side verification? | FSVP or other importer compliance workflow | That the shipment-data handoff to the filer was complete |

This is why prior notice should stay in its own lane. When teams say, "the supplier is already FDA registered," or "the broker has the documents," they often skip the separate question of whether the exact phosphatidylserine shipment data is ready for a valid prior-notice submission.

### One weak broker handoff can delay an otherwise acceptable shipment

The filing timing rules make this even more important. Under 21 CFR 1.279, prior notice generally must be confirmed by FDA for review at least **2 hours before road arrival**, **4 hours before rail arrival**, **4 hours before air arrival**, or **8 hours before water arrival**. The same rule says that if the filing goes through ACE, it may not be submitted more than **30 calendar days** before anticipated arrival, while PNSI filings may not be submitted more than **15 calendar days** before anticipated arrival.

That means a prior-notice problem often shows up late, when the shipment itself is already moving correctly. The product may be acceptable. The COA may be clean. The packaging may be fine. But if the filer receives incomplete or outdated shipment data, the arrival-control file can still fail.

The consequence is not theoretical. FDA's regulations state that food can be refused admission for **no prior notice**, **inaccurate prior notice**, or **untimely prior notice**. If that happens, the shipment can be held at the port of entry or another secure facility unless CBP and FDA direct otherwise. For a B2B buyer, that is a strong reason to treat prior notice as a controlled pre-arrival handoff, not as a last-minute broker detail.

## The Six-Part Workflow for Prior Notice and Entry-File Handoff

### Lock the exact phosphatidylserine route and shipment setup first

Before a filer receives anything, the buyer should write down what shipment is actually under review. Is the product general phosphatidylserine, a soy route, or a sunflower route? Which legal seller and manufacturing route are tied to the shipment? Which mode of transport is being used? Which U.S. port of arrival is expected? Who is the importer, owner, and ultimate consignee for this movement?

This step matters because prior notice is shipment-specific. If one team is still discussing a soy route while another has moved to a sunflower route, or if the arrival plan changed from air to ocean, the prior-notice file can drift even while the commercial file still looks familiar.

At minimum, the route note should usually identify:

- exact phosphatidylserine route under shipment
- legal seller and relevant ship-from route
- mode of transport and expected arrival pattern
- port of arrival
- importer, owner, and ultimate consignee
- who will transmit the filing

Buyers should still confirm the exact order configuration, consignee structure, and arrival plan before the notice is filed.

### Decide who will submit prior notice and which filing path will be used

The next step is ownership. Under 21 CFR 1.278, prior notice may be submitted by any person with knowledge of the required information, and that person may use another person to transmit it. FDA's filing page explains that prior notice can be filed through the CBP Automated Broker Interface / Automated Commercial Environment path or through FDA's Prior Notice System Interface.

That means import teams should not assume the broker automatically owns every decision. Someone still has to own the data. In practice, buyers should answer these questions before the clock starts:

1. Who is the submitter with knowledge of the required information?
2. Who is the transmitter if the submitter is using another party?
3. Will this shipment be filed through ACE or through PNSI?
4. Which team will confirm that the filer is working from the current shipment facts rather than an older template?

This is one of the most common failure points in U.S. ingredient imports. A commercial file gets copied forward from the last order, but the actual arrival mode, consignee, or route data has changed. The team thinks the broker is "handling prior notice," when in reality the broker is waiting on accurate shipment inputs.

### Build one controlled data sheet before broker filing starts

Once ownership is clear, build one shipment-data sheet that can be checked before filing. Under 21 CFR 1.281 and the related eCFR filing requirements, prior notice can require information such as the article of food identity, manufacturer, country from which shipped, importer, owner, ultimate consignee, mode of transportation, carrier information, and anticipated arrival information.

For buyers, the most useful approach is a simple pre-filing table:

| Data area | Why the team should confirm it before filing |
| --- | --- |
| Exact product identity | Prevents a generic "PS powder" description from being reused for the wrong route |
| Manufacturer and ship-from data | Keeps the filing tied to the actual supply path being imported |
| Importer, owner, and ultimate consignee | Prevents responsibility gaps between purchaser, distributor, and destination party |
| Mode, carrier, and arrival details | Keeps the notice aligned with the real transport plan and timing window |
| Port-of-arrival logic | Helps avoid a filing based on a different arrival route than the one now booked |

The key discipline is not to let this table become a substitute for your technical file. It is a shipment-control sheet. It should be consistent with the same product and commercial records already under approval, but it should not be confused with the specification, COA, [documents checklist](/resources/documents-for-ps-ingredients/), or internal [incoming inspection workflow](/resources/phosphatidylserine-incoming-inspection-warehouse-release/).

### Keep prior notice separate from supplier qualification and technical release

This is where many teams over-compress the file. Prior notice is one arrival-control step. Supplier qualification, lot release, and technical acceptance are different steps.

For example, a U.S. supplement manufacturer might still need separate work on supplier qualification and identity testing. A distributor may still need a customer-facing document set. QA may still be reviewing the current COA path or technical statements. Those decisions should not be merged into the prior-notice question.

The clean internal language is simple:

- prior notice answers whether the shipment-arrival notification is being filed correctly
- supplier approval answers whether the business relationship is acceptable
- technical review answers whether the exact phosphatidylserine route and batch evidence are acceptable
- receiving and release answer whether the goods can move into stock or production

That separation reduces confusion when different teams ask different questions at the same time. ### Refile when key information changes after confirmation

This is the operational rule many buyers miss. Under 21 CFR 1.282, if required prior-notice information changes after FDA has confirmed the filing for review, the filer generally must **resubmit prior notice** unless the food will no longer be imported. The regulation specifically carves out limited exceptions for changes such as quantity, anticipated arrival information, or planned-shipment information, but the safest commercial practice is still to treat any meaningful shipment-data change as a recheck event before the goods arrive.

The buyer-side lesson is straightforward. Do **not** assume that one prior-notice confirmation number remains usable after the shipment facts materially change. Recheck the file if:

- the product route changed
- the manufacturer or ship-from path changed
- the importer, owner, or ultimate consignee changed
- the filing method or carrier path changed
- the team is no longer sure the filer is using the current data set

This matters because FDA's consequence rule states that imported food can be refused for missing, inaccurate, or untimely prior notice. That is a preventable failure if teams treat shipment changes as filing-control changes rather than as "small logistics updates."

### Close the shipment file with a go, hold, or escalation note

The last step is to close the file in language the next person can use.

- **Go:** the exact route, shipment data, filing path, and responsible submitter are aligned well enough to proceed.
- **Hold:** the shipment may still be commercially acceptable, but the filer lacks current or complete prior-notice data.
- **Escalate:** the route, consignee, or arrival structure changed enough that the team should stop and rebuild the handoff before filing.

This short note prevents repeat confusion later. If the shipment is delayed, the importer changes, or the broker asks again for confirmation, everyone can see whether the prior-notice file was truly ready or only assumed to be ready.

## FAQ

### Is prior notice the same as FDA approval for a phosphatidylserine supplier?

No. Prior notice is an advance-notification control for imported food shipments. It does not by itself prove supplier qualification, product approval, or technical release.

### Who can submit prior notice for an imported phosphatidylserine shipment?

Under 21 CFR 1.278, any person with knowledge of the required information may submit prior notice, and that person may use another person to transmit the information on their behalf.

### What timing should U.S. buyers watch most closely?

The minimum timing depends on arrival mode. Under 21 CFR 1.279, prior notice generally must be confirmed by FDA for review at least 2 hours before road arrival, 4 hours before rail or air arrival, and 8 hours before water arrival.

### Does one prior-notice confirmation number stay valid if the shipment details change?

Not automatically. Under 21 CFR 1.282, changes to required prior-notice information can require the filer to resubmit prior notice, so buyers should recheck the file whenever the shipment facts materially change.

### What happens if prior notice is missing, inaccurate, or late?

FDA's regulations say food may be refused admission for no prior notice, inaccurate prior notice, or untimely prior notice. In those cases, the shipment can be held at the port of entry or another secure location unless CBP and FDA direct otherwise.
