## Why NDI Review Is Its Own Buyer Task

### NDI status is not the same as import, label, or supplier approval

U.S. supplement teams often collect several files at once: [prior notice planning](/resources/phosphatidylserine-prior-notice-checklist-us/), [FDA food facility registration review](/resources/phosphatidylserine-fda-food-facility-registration-us/), [Supplement Facts routing](/resources/phosphatidylserine-supplement-facts-label-us/), [Proposition 65 review](/resources/phosphatidylserine-proposition-65-statement-us/), and [supplier COA qualification](/resources/phosphatidylserine-supplier-coa-qualification-us/). Those are all legitimate buyer tasks, but they answer different questions.

An ingredient can have a usable import file and still have an unresolved NDI issue. A supplement label can look complete and still sit on top of an uncertain ingredient-status file. A supplier can provide current specifications, batch records, and packaging details while the marketer still lacks a documented basis for its NDI position. That is why NDI review deserves its own decision lane.

### There is no official pre-1994 phosphatidylserine list to rely on

FDA's background page for industry states two points that matter immediately for phosphatidylserine buyers:

- an NDI is a dietary ingredient not marketed in the United States before October 15, 1994
- there is no authoritative list of dietary ingredients marketed in dietary supplements before that date

That means teams should stop asking for a mythical one-line certificate proving old-ingredient status. They need a route-specific evidence file instead. This is especially important for phosphatidylserine because the commercial label "PS" may hide meaningful differences in source, composition, carrier, concentration, or manufacturing path. A general phosphatidylserine record is not automatically the same thing as a soy PS or sunflower PS record for the exact form under launch review.

FDA also states that if you are unsure whether an ingredient is an NDI, you may still submit a notification. That does not mean every project should file automatically. It means uncertainty should be handled as a documented regulatory decision, not as an informal email assumption.

## A Six-Step Workflow for Phosphatidylserine NDI Review

### Lock the exact phosphatidylserine route and finished use first

Start with the exact sellable route. Record whether the project uses [general phosphatidylserine](/products/phosphatidylserine/), [soy phosphatidylserine](/products/soy-phosphatidylserine/), or [sunflower phosphatidylserine](/products/sunflower-phosphatidylserine/). Add the intended dosage form, recommended daily intake, serving count, target consumer, and whether the product will be sold only as a dietary supplement rather than as a conventional food concept.

This matters because the NDI question is tied to the ingredient and the conditions of use. FDA's 21 CFR 190.6 rule requires any premarket notification to describe the supplement or supplements that will contain the NDI, including the ingredient level and the conditions of use recommended or suggested in labeling. A buyer cannot evaluate that cleanly if the route is still drifting between multiple PS forms.

### Separate supplier support from the legal marketing decision

The supplier can support the file, but the finished-product marketer owns the decision. For a Nutranexa project, that means the supplier may help with route identification, specification logic, COA examples, packaging facts, and manufacturing context, while the brand, contract manufacturer, or distributor still owns the U.S. regulatory conclusion for the finished supplement. Without that ownership split, procurement often assumes regulatory has enough, while regulatory assumes the supplier already settled the issue.

### Build the status evidence file before debating conclusions

The strongest NDI reviews begin with evidence collection, not opinions. FDA's background page says manufacturers and distributors are responsible for determining whether an ingredient is an NDI and for documenting the basis for that conclusion. That supports a three-lane evidence file:

1. evidence of marketing in U.S. dietary supplements before October 15, 1994
2. evidence relevant to the food-supply and same-form analysis
3. evidence relevant to a potential NDIN if the first two lanes do not close the question

FDA's public [Submitted 75-Day Premarket Notifications for New Dietary Ingredients](https://www.fda.gov/food/new-dietary-ingredient-ndi-notification-process/submitted-75-day-premarket-notifications-new-dietary-ingredients) page is useful because it provides a searchable list of publicly displayable NDINs by ingredient name, submitter, submission date, and response date. FDA's [ingredient directory](https://www.fda.gov/food/dietary-supplements/information-select-dietary-supplement-ingredients-and-other-substances) is also useful because it brings together FDA actions and communications for ingredients found in products marketed as dietary supplements. Neither page is a substitute for route matching, but both are legitimate research checkpoints.

### If notification may be required, map the filing package early

Once the team sees that a notification may be needed, timing becomes operational, not theoretical. FDA's NDI process page states that the notification generally must include the basis on which the manufacturer or distributor has concluded that a dietary supplement containing the NDI will reasonably be expected to be safe under the conditions of use. The current FDA submission page also says notifications can be filed electronically through COSM.

The regulation at 21 CFR 190.6 requires the package to include:

- the name and complete address of the manufacturer or distributor
- the name of the new dietary ingredient
- a description of the supplement or supplements that contain it
- the ingredient level in the product
- the conditions of use recommended or suggested in labeling
- the history of use or other evidence of safety
- supporting reprints or copies of published references
- English translations for foreign-language materials
- the submitter's signature

The same regulation also states that the filing must be made at least 75 days before interstate marketing, substantive additional information can reset the filing date, FDA keeps the notification confidential for 90 days after filing, and failure of FDA to respond does not mean the ingredient is safe or not adulterated. If a notification path is possible, do not wait until labels, production slots, and customer launch dates are already locked.

### Keep the NDI file aligned with the finished supplement record

An NDI review can become internally inconsistent if the ingredient file says one thing and the finished supplement says another. The route, daily use, dosage form, and responsible marketer should stay aligned across the NDI file, the [Supplement Facts review](/resources/phosphatidylserine-supplement-facts-label-us/), formulation records, supplier approval notes, and any downstream customer questionnaire.

That is especially important when teams change:

- the PS source route
- the daily serving
- the dosage form
- the target consumer
- the co-manufacturer or brand owner

Each of those changes can reopen an earlier conclusion. A route-specific decision that looked defensible for one supplement configuration may not carry cleanly into another.

### Close with a go, hold, or escalate decision

The final step is to close the review in writing:

- **Go:** the exact route is stable, the evidence file supports the position, and launch timing matches the team's conclusion.
- **Hold:** the project may still move forward later, but the evidence is too thin or too mismatched to support a clean launch now.
- **Escalate:** the team needs outside regulatory or legal review, a submitter decision, or a formal NDIN path before anyone treats the project as market-ready.

This closeout matters because the NDI question often resurfaces late, usually when the first commercial batch or customer launch calendar is already moving. One written status line is more useful than a long chain of scattered comments.

## A Practical Evidence Matrix for PS NDI Review

| Evidence lane | What it can help answer | What it does not prove by itself |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Pre-1994 marketing evidence | Whether the team has a basis to argue the dietary ingredient was marketed in U.S. supplements before October 15, 1994 | That every modern source, carrier, or form of PS fits the same conclusion |
| Food-supply evidence | Whether the team has support for the same-form analysis under FDA's framework | That a conventional-food record automatically clears every supplement route |
| Public NDIN list | Whether related public notifications exist and how FDA records them | That your own product is covered by another party's filing |
| FDA ingredient directory | What FDA actions or communications are publicly linked to the ingredient name | That the ingredient is approved, exempt, or settled for your exact route |
| Supplier specification and COA | What exact route is being quoted and how it is described technically | Whether the marketer has closed the NDI obligation |
| Finished supplement records | The actual daily serving, dosage form, and labeling context | Whether the ingredient-status evidence is sufficient |

## Common NDI Mistakes to Prevent

1. Treating a supplier email or brochure as if it closes the NDI question.
2. Assuming any public reference to phosphatidylserine proves the exact route was marketed in U.S. supplements before October 15, 1994.
3. Mixing food-supply evidence, import paperwork, and finished-label review into one uncontrolled folder.
4. Using a public NDIN or ingredient-directory entry as if it automatically covers another company's product.
5. Forgetting that the 75-day timeline runs from filing, and that substantive amendments can reset the filing date.
6. Treating FDA silence as product approval.
7. Letting the formula, serving, or source route change after the NDI position was documented without reopening the file.

The practical fix is narrow: keep one question at the center of the review. **What is the exact phosphatidylserine route, what evidence supports its status for this supplement use, and who owns the final U.S. marketing decision?**

## FAQ

### Does phosphatidylserine automatically avoid NDI notification in the United States?

No. FDA says manufacturers and distributors are responsible for determining whether an ingredient is an NDI and for documenting the basis for that conclusion. The answer depends on the exact route, evidence file, and intended use.

### Is a supplier COA or specification enough for phosphatidylserine NDI review?

No. They help define the route under review, but they do not by themselves prove pre-1994 marketing, same-form food-supply status, or satisfaction of a notification obligation.

### If a public NDIN exists for phosphatidylserine, does it automatically cover my product?

Not automatically. Public NDIN records are research checkpoints. Buyers still need to compare exact ingredient identity, source, form, conditions of use, and marketer role before treating another filing as relevant.

### Does FDA silence after an NDI filing mean the product is approved?

No. FDA's regulation states that failure of the agency to respond does not constitute a finding that the ingredient or supplement is safe or not adulterated.
