Phosphatidylserine Customs Clearance Checklist for European Importers

Prepare phosphatidylserine customs clearance in Europe by aligning EORI, product identity, CN classification, value, origin, and food-import controls.

Published July 22, 2026

Посмотреть продукты PSЗапросить характеристики

European buyers should prepare phosphatidylserine customs clearance as a controlled shipment file, not as a last-minute exchange of a commercial invoice and a suggested tariff code. The importer needs an EORI-linked declaration path, an exact product description, a defensible classification review, value and origin support, aligned packing and transport data, and a check of any food-import measures that apply to the specific product and country of origin.

The key caution is simple: do not assume that every phosphatidylserine powder has the same customs classification or import-control path. General phosphatidylserine, soy phosphatidylserine, and sunflower phosphatidylserine may differ in source, composition, concentration, carrier system, and commercial presentation. Those facts can matter when an importer or customs representative reviews the Combined Nomenclature and TARIC measures.

This resource gives European ingredient importers, distributors, supplement manufacturers, procurement managers, logistics teams, and quality teams a practical pre-arrival workflow. It does not assign a CN code, calculate duty, or replace advice from the competent customs authority or appointed representative. It shows which facts should be stable before anyone makes those decisions.

The Short Answer for EU Importers

Before a phosphatidylserine shipment reaches the EU, the buyer should be able to answer six questions in one file: who is the importer and declarant, which EORI is being used, what exact product is in the drums, how the proposed CN/TARIC classification was supported, how customs value and origin were documented, and whether any food-import control applies to that exact route and origin.

The European Commission states that an EORI number is mandatory for customs clearance operations in the EU. It also explains that goods declared to EU customs must be classified under the Combined Nomenclature or a nomenclature based on it; classification determines both duty treatment and non-tariff measures. The supplier can provide product evidence, but the importer and its customs professionals still need to own the declaration decision.

For a clean handoff, keep the customs file connected to?but separate from?the phosphatidylserine specification and COA review. The specification supports product identity. The COA supports a batch review. Neither one, by itself, completes the customs declaration, valuation, origin, or import-control assessment.

Why Customs Clearance Needs Its Own PS File

Customs, food compliance, and quality approval are separate decisions

Customs release, food-law readiness, and internal QA release may happen around the same shipment, but they do not answer the same question. Customs needs declaration data and supporting documents. Food and regulatory teams need to confirm the intended market route and any applicable restrictions or official controls. QA needs the approved specification, current batch COA, receiving criteria, and document status.

Combining everything into one uncontrolled folder creates two risks. Entry-critical facts can be hidden among unrelated certificates, while quality teams may assume customs release means the ingredient has passed internal approval. A better approach is three linked lanes: the customs and broker packet, the food-compliance file, and the QA receiving file.

The European Commission notes that most food of non-animal origin is not automatically channelled through a specific border entity, but certain products and origins can be subject to increased controls or special measures. That is why a general statement such as ?plant-derived food ingredient? is not enough. The team must check the current rule set for the exact goods and origin rather than assuming either automatic certification or automatic exemption.

A generic ingredient name is not enough for classification

?PS powder? may work in an early sales conversation, but it is too vague for a controlled customs review. A classification file should distinguish the exact product route and record the facts a broker or customs authority may need: ingredient name, biological source, phosphatidylserine concentration or commercial grade, other constituents or carriers, physical form, intended commercial use, packaging unit, and manufacturer identity shown on the transaction documents.

The EU Classification Information System explains that classification determines the customs-duty rate and non-tariff measures. It brings together CN information, TARIC information, classification regulations, committee conclusions, and court rulings. The point is not that buyers must become tariff specialists. The point is that the classification proposal should be connected to verifiable product facts rather than copied from an old invoice or a different supplier's material.

A Seven-Step EU Customs-Clearance Workflow

Confirm the importer, declarant, representative, and EORI

Start with the parties, not the documents. Record which legal entity will import the goods, who will lodge the customs declaration, whether representation is direct or indirect, and which EORI will identify the relevant operator. Confirm the destination Member State and the customs office or port path with the appointed representative.

The European Commission describes EORI as the common identifier for customs operations and states that EU-established economic operators need one. A non-EU operator may also need an EORI when it carries out specified customs activities. Do not wait for vessel or flight arrival to discover that the named importer, invoice buyer, consignee, representative, and EORI record do not align.

Your opening shipment note should therefore name the importer, declarant, customs representative, consignee, delivery location, EORI owner, and internal escalation contact.

Lock the exact commercial product identity

Next, freeze one route-specific identity statement. Is the shipment general phosphatidylserine, soy phosphatidylserine, or sunflower phosphatidylserine? What grade or concentration is being sold? Does the commercial material include another ingredient or carrier? What physical form is shipped, and how is each package described?

Match this identity across the quotation, purchase order, commercial invoice, packing list, specification, and broker data sheet. If one file says ?soy PS,? another says only ?food additive,? and a third uses a general phosphatidylserine description, pause and resolve the mismatch. A clean description should be accurate without making unsupported regulatory claims or using a tariff code as the product name.

Review CN and TARIC classification without guessing a code

Once identity is stable, give the customs representative the evidence needed to review classification. This typically includes the current specification, composition or grade description, source, manufacturing description at an appropriate level, physical form, intended use, and a representative product image or sample if requested.

Use the Commission's CLASS and TARIC resources to research the current framework, but treat a search result as research?not a binding decision. The Commission states that Binding Tariff Information is a legal classification decision, generally valid for three years across the EU, and that an application must describe the goods accurately and completely. A BTI may be worth discussing with the importer or customs authority when classification uncertainty is material, recurring, or commercially significant.

Do not publish or reuse a CN code in the internal master data until its scope matches the exact product being imported. A code previously used for a different concentration, carrier system, formulation, or commercial presentation is evidence to investigate, not proof.

Align the invoice, packing list, and transport description

The transaction documents should tell the same shipment story. Check the legal seller and buyer, invoice number and date, Incoterm used by the parties, currency, unit price, total price, quantity, net and gross weight, number and type of packages, package marks, origin statement, and product description.

Then compare the packing list and bill of lading or air waybill. Drum count, pallet count, weights, consignee, and product wording should reconcile. Nutranexa's current PS pages state a 25 kg minimum order quantity and 25 kg net per drum for phosphatidylserine. Buyers should still confirm the exact order's packing and shipment details before booking rather than treating a website baseline as the final transport record.

Build the valuation and origin evidence

Classification is only one customs pillar. The European Commission identifies classification, origin, and customs value as core inputs for duty treatment. Its customs-valuation guidance says the main valuation method is transaction value?the price paid or payable?subject to specified additions and deductions under the Union Customs Code.

Create a valuation worksheet or broker note that connects the invoice price to freight, insurance, commissions, assists, royalties, or other relevant elements where applicable. Do not let procurement's purchase price, the carrier's freight figure, and the broker's declared value develop independently.

Keep origin separate from shipping country. Record the country of origin basis being used, the manufacturing entity, and any preferential-origin claim under review. If preferential treatment is requested, obtain the correct origin evidence for the applicable arrangement; do not infer preference from a ?made in? line or route through a particular port.

Check food-import controls for the exact route and origin

Before dispatch, ask the regulatory or customs owner to check current EU and Member State requirements for the exact product, origin, and intended use. Confirm whether the goods fall within any increased-control list, emergency measure, certificate requirement, pre-notification path, or national implementation step.

The Commission explains that most non-animal-origin food is not routinely channelled through border control entities, while certain product-origin combinations can be listed for increased controls and the relevant lists are reviewed regularly. The practical rule is therefore neither ?a certificate is always required? nor ?no certificate is required.? The right answer must be checked close to shipment using current classifications and measures.

This review should connect to the buyer's wider European supplier and document qualification rather than duplicate it.

Close with a pre-arrival go, hold, or escalation decision

Complete the file before cargo arrival with one recorded status:

  • Go: parties, EORI, identity, classification basis, value, origin, transaction documents, and applicable import controls are aligned.
  • Hold: a correctable document or data gap remains, and shipment release should not be assumed.
  • Escalate: classification, representation, origin, value, or food-control applicability is uncertain enough to require customs-authority, broker, legal, or regulatory input.

The owner should record the decision date, open items, responsible person, and next checkpoint. This is more useful than a long email thread because warehouse, finance, QA, and the customs representative can all see the same release position.

Common Clearance Mistakes to Prevent

Most avoidable problems begin with one team assuming another team has validated the same fact.

  1. Copying a CN code from an earlier shipment without checking whether the source, concentration, carrier, or presentation changed.
  2. Using ?phosphatidylserine? on one document, ?soy PS? on another, and a different commercial description in the broker master data.
  3. Treating the supplier specification as a complete customs packet.
  4. Discovering after booking that the importer, consignee, representative, or EORI owner is not the entity expected by the declaration plan.
  5. Equating shipping country with origin or assuming a preference without valid supporting evidence.
  6. Assuming plant-derived food never triggers a specific EU import control.
  7. Letting the invoice value move forward without reconciling freight, insurance, and other valuation inputs.

The best prevention is a short pre-arrival review built around evidence, ownership, and one decision status.

Часто задаваемые вопросы

What documents are needed to import phosphatidylserine into the EU?

The exact legal set depends on the shipment and Member State, but a practical file normally aligns the customs declaration inputs, EORI and party details, commercial invoice, packing list, transport document, product identity evidence, classification support, customs-value inputs, origin evidence, and any food-import documents applicable to the exact product-origin route.

Does an EU phosphatidylserine importer need an EORI number?

Yes, an EORI is mandatory for customs-clearance operations in the EU. The team should confirm which operator owns the EORI and how the importer, declarant, consignee, and customs representative fit the declaration structure before dispatch.

What CN code should be used for phosphatidylserine?

This article does not assign a CN code. Classification should be reviewed against the exact source, composition, concentration or grade, physical form, commercial presentation, and intended use. CLASS and TARIC support research, while a Binding Tariff Information decision can provide legal certainty for the product described in the decision.

Does plant-derived phosphatidylserine always need a border-control certificate?

No universal answer should be assumed. The Commission states that most food of non-animal origin is not routinely channelled through border control entities, but specific product-origin combinations can be subject to increased controls or special measures. Check the current rules for the exact route before shipment.

Can a specification and COA replace the customs broker packet?

No. They support product and batch review, but they do not by themselves establish the parties, EORI path, declaration data, tariff classification, customs value, origin claim, or transaction-document alignment needed for clearance.

Conclusion

Phosphatidylserine customs clearance in Europe becomes more predictable when buyers control the shipment facts before cargo arrival. Confirm the parties and EORI, lock the exact PS identity, support classification without copying an old code, reconcile invoice and transport data, document value and origin, and check current food-import measures for the actual route.

The final test is practical: can the importer and its representative explain what the goods are, who is declaring them, why the classification is defensible, how value and origin were supported, and which current import controls were checked? If any answer is still based on assumption, hold or escalate before dispatch.
